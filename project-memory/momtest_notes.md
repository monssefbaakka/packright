# PackRight: Mom Test Interview Simulations

---

## Interview 1: The Reluctant Organizer

**Background:** 26 year old who always ends up planning trips for her friend group.

---

**Interviewer:** When was the last time you planned a trip with a group of friends?

**Organizer:** Just last month. Six people, four days in Smoky Mountains. I basically ran the whole thing.

**Interviewer:** What did that look like for you?

**Organizer:** A lot of back and forth on WhatsApp honestly. I would ask people what they are bringing and half of them would not even reply until the day before. I ended up just telling everyone what to bring because it was easier than waiting.

**Interviewer:** Did that work out?

**Organizer:** Mostly. But we still showed up with four portable chargers and no one brought a can opener. Small things but it is annoying when you could have avoided it.

**Interviewer:** How did you figure out what the group needed to bring in the first place?

**Organizer:** I just sat down and made a list in my notes app from scratch. Which took forever because I had to think of everything myself. I wish there was some way to just tell it where I am going and for how long and it spits out a list for me.

**Interviewer:** Once you had the list, how did you share it and get people on board?

**Organizer:** I sent it in the group chat. But before that I had to figure out who was even coming because not everyone in our friend circle joins every trip. So I was texting people individually, waiting for replies, and then manually pulling them into the right thread.

**Interviewer:** Did that ever cause any confusion?

**Organizer:** More than once. I have two friends with the same first name saved in my phone and I once looped in the completely wrong person into a trip planning thread. She had no idea what was going on. And for people I do not have saved I had to ask someone else for their number first before I could even add them. It just adds so many unnecessary steps before you even get to the actual planning.

**Interviewer:** How would you have preferred to handle that?

**Organizer:** If people just had some kind of unique username or handle I could search for, I could look them up and add them directly. No digging through contacts, no adding the wrong person, no asking around for numbers. Just find them and pull them in.

**Interviewer:** Once you had everyone, how did item assignment go?

**Organizer:** I asked people to reply with what they would bring. Some people claimed the same thing, some things nobody claimed. I had to manually follow up with everyone.

**Interviewer:** What did you do with the items nobody claimed?

**Organizer:** I either brought them myself or just assigned them to someone randomly over chat. It was awkward because nobody wants to feel like they are being bossed around.

**Interviewer:** How did you know if someone actually packed what they said they would?

**Organizer:** I did not. I just trusted them. And sometimes that was a mistake.

**Interviewer:** If you could change one thing about how your group coordinates packing, what would it be?

**Organizer:** Some way where everyone can see what is covered and what is not without me having to be the one sending follow up messages. And I want to know they actually packed it, not just said they would.

---

**Narrative Summary**

She has been the default trip organizer for her friend group for years and the frustration is not packing itself but the enormous coordination overhead that surrounds it. Every trip starts with building a list from scratch in her notes app, trying to remember everything the group might need entirely on her own. Even before that, getting the right people into the same thread is painful. She described adding the wrong person because of duplicate contact names and chasing down phone numbers through mutual friends just to reach someone she wanted to include. She directly expressed wanting a way to search for someone by a unique handle and add them instantly without any of that friction.

Once the list was out, items got double claimed or sat unclaimed with no structured way to handle either situation. Assigning leftovers over chat felt socially uncomfortable and she described it as people not wanting to feel bossed around. The deepest frustration came at the end — even after people said they would bring something, she had no way to know if they actually did until the group was standing somewhere without it. She had been let down enough times that she stopped trusting verbal confirmations entirely and wanted a way to know people actually packed what they committed to.

## Interview 2: The Casual Participant

**Background:** 28 year old who joins trips planned by others and prefers a minimal role.

---

**Interviewer:** How often do you travel with a group?

**Participant:** A few times a year. My friends usually put it together and I just join.

**Interviewer:** What does joining usually look like for you when it comes to packing?

**Participant:** Someone sends a list in the chat and says hey can you bring this. I say sure. Done.

**Interviewer:** Does that always work smoothly?

**Participant:** Not really. Sometimes two of us bring the same thing because we both said yes to it in the chat and nobody was tracking. Last trip both me and my friend brought a bluetooth speaker. Waste of bag space.

**Interviewer:** What about things nobody ends up bringing?

**Participant:** That happens too. We get there and realize nobody brought sunscreen or something basic. Someone has to go buy it which is annoying.

**Interviewer:** When you say yes to bringing something, how do you decide if you should bring it or not?

**Participant:** I just go off the list and pick whatever feels manageable. But I have no idea what others have already said they would bring. The chat moves fast and I am not scrolling back through everything. So it is basically a guess.

**Interviewer:** Has anything ever gone wrong with something the group was supposed to share between everyone?

**Participant:** Yeah, like snacks or water for a big group. One person cannot carry enough for everyone but nobody ever coordinates how many people are pitching in. Everyone just assumes someone else handled the rest of it.

**Interviewer:** How does that usually play out?

**Participant:** You find out when you get there. Which is not ideal.

**Interviewer:** When something like that happens, is there ever any confusion about who was supposed to handle it?

**Participant:** Always. Nobody can point to a specific person because nobody actually tracked who said what. And it gets messier when not everyone in the group knows each other. Someone adds a friend of a friend and half the group does not even know who that person is. People are like who is this number. It makes it even harder to trust that they will follow through on something.

**Interviewer:** Does that come up often?

**Participant:** More than you would think. And when you do not know who someone is, you are less likely to assign them something important. Having a name and maybe a photo attached would make things feel more real and accountable.

**Interviewer:** Once you pack something, do you let the group know?

**Participant:** Sometimes I send a message saying I am all set. But it gets buried in the chat within minutes and I am not sure anyone even sees it.

---

**Narrative Summary**

He is a willing participant but operates with almost no visibility into what the rest of the group is doing. The duplicate item problem surfaced immediately and without prompting — two people bringing the same bluetooth speaker because nobody was tracking confirmations. Gaps were equally common, with items nobody claimed simply going missing until the group arrived and realized the problem. He described this as everyone assuming someone else handled it, which is a direct consequence of having no shared view of what is actually covered. A more subtle version of the same problem came up around shared items like snacks or water, where one person cannot carry enough for everyone but nobody coordinates how many people are pitching in or whether the total is sufficient.

The identity and accountability point emerged naturally from the conversation about unclear ownership — when nobody can point to who was responsible, confusion about who is even in the group makes it worse. He drew a direct connection between not recognizing someone in a shared thread and not trusting them with a responsibility, and described a named profile with a photo as something that would make coordination feel more real. His own signal of readiness after packing gets buried in chat within minutes, reflecting a broader problem where individual confirmations are completely invisible at the group level.

## Interview 3: The Anxious Over-Packer

**Background:** 24 year old who overpacks out of fear of forgetting something important.

---

**Interviewer:** Tell me about the last group trip you went on.

**Over-Packer:** Beach trip with five friends a couple months ago. It was fun but I way overpacked as usual.

**Interviewer:** What does way overpacking look like for you?

**Over-Packer:** I brought my own first aid kit, snacks, two extra towels, a portable fan. Half of it I never touched because other people brought the same things.

**Interviewer:** Why do you think that happens?

**Over-Packer:** Because I have no idea what everyone else is bringing. I see a packing list in the chat but I do not actually know who confirmed what. So I just bring everything I think we might need just to be safe.

**Interviewer:** Does it cause any problems beyond extra weight?

**Over-Packer:** It is embarrassing a little. My friends joke about my suitcase. And it is just unnecessary stress. I spend way too much time packing and second guessing myself.

**Interviewer:** What would make you feel comfortable enough to leave something out of your bag?

**Over-Packer:** If I could actually see that someone else confirmed they are bringing it and they packed it. Not just that they said they would. Because people say they will and then forget.

**Interviewer:** Has that happened before?

**Over-Packer:** Yes. Someone said they would bring a first aid kit and they forgot it entirely. So now I always bring one regardless of what anyone says.

**Interviewer:** What goes through your head when someone tells you they will bring something?

**Over-Packer:** Honestly not much confidence. Saying you will bring something means nothing until it is in your bag. People have good intentions and then life happens and they forget. I have been the person who showed up without something I said I would bring too. So I just do not rely on it anymore.

**Interviewer:** Is there anything that would change that for you?

**Over-Packer:** If there was some way for the whole group to see in real time what is actually packed and what is still outstanding, I would trust that. Like if I can see the group is at 90 percent ready and the first aid kit is already packed by someone, I would leave mine at home. But right now I have no way of knowing that so I just bring everything.

---

**Narrative Summary**

Her over-packing is not a habit or a personality quirk. It is a rational response to having no reliable information about what the group has genuinely prepared. She sees the chat list, she sees people reply, but she has no way of knowing whether any of it translates into something actually being in someone's bag on the day of the trip. The first aid kit story was the clearest validation across all three interviews — someone explicitly said they would bring it and showed up without it, permanently changing how she approaches packing. She now brings critical items regardless of what anyone says because past experience has taught her that verbal confirmation means very little.

She drew a sharp and unprompted distinction between claiming an item and actually packing it, even acknowledging she had been on the wrong side of that gap herself. What she described wanting was not a reminder but a live view of what the group has collectively packed so her decisions are based on verified reality rather than optimistic chat replies. Her specific example of seeing a percentage readiness for the group before deciding what to leave behind was the most direct articulation of the readiness dashboard across all three conversations.

## Consolidated Pain Points

The following problems emerged independently and consistently across all three interviews:

Building a packing list from scratch for every trip is tedious and time consuming with no starting point based on where the group is going or for how long.

There is no clean way to find and add group members to a shared planning space, creating confusion especially when friends of friends are involved and nobody knows who is who.

A display name and recognizable identity attached to each person makes group coordination feel more accountable and trustworthy, and the absence of it breaks that trust entirely.

There is no way to know who is bringing what or how many people have claimed the same item, making duplicates and gaps an inevitable outcome of chat based coordination.

For shared items that require multiple contributors, there is no way to know how many people are needed versus how many have already committed, making it impossible to know when an item is truly covered.

Unclaimed items either get forgotten or awkwardly assigned by the organizer over chat with no clean mechanism and no real accountability attached to the assignment.

The organizer carries a disproportionate coordination burden including building the list, following up with people, assigning unclaimed items, and checking whether anyone actually packed what they said they would.

Claiming an item over chat provides no accountability and no way to verify if someone actually packed it, making claimed status an unreliable signal the group cannot act on.

There is no shared view of group readiness that everyone can see in real time without digging through chat history, leaving each person to make packing decisions in isolation.

The gap between claiming an item and actually packing it is the most critical and consistently untracked moment in the entire group packing process.
